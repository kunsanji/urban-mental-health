Four Questions We Asked About Urban Mental Health

We looked at four big questions using PHQ‑4 scores and survey information on income, education, access to care, and smoking. Tap each card to see what we found.

Question 1
How do mental health scores vary by income and education?
Big gap by income
What we did: We compared average PHQ‑4 scores across income brackets and education levels.
▶ Tap for details
  • Key insight Residents in the lowest‑income group had the highest PHQ‑4 scores, meaning more anxiety and depressive symptoms. Those in the highest‑income group had the lowest scores.
  • Education Mental health scores were similar across education levels. Schooling alone did not show a strong link to PHQ‑4 scores in this sample.
In simple terms: Money stress showed up more clearly than schooling in our mental health data.
Question 2
Does access to mental health services predict PHQ‑4 scores?
Easy → Difficult
What we did: We ran a simple linear regression using PHQ‑4 score and a 0–2 scale of how difficult people felt it was to access mental health care.
▶ Tap for details
  • Result The model was not statistically significant. People who said access was “difficult” did not have consistently higher PHQ‑4 scores than those who said it was “easy.”
  • Why this matters Access still matters, but in this dataset it did not explain most of the differences in mental health. Other pressures—like money, work, family, or stigma—may be playing a larger role.
Takeaway: Making services easier to reach is important, but it is not the only answer. We also have to reduce the everyday stresses that push people toward crisis.
Question 3
Do smokers have different mental health scores than non‑smokers?
Smokers vs. non‑smokers
What we did: We used an independent t‑test to compare PHQ‑4 scores between smokers and non‑smokers.
▶ Tap for details
  • Result Smokers had slightly higher PHQ‑4 scores on average, but the difference was small and not statistically significant.
  • Context Smoking is still a health risk, and stress may influence both smoking and mood. But in this dataset, smoking alone did not clearly separate people with higher or lower mental health scores.
Takeaway: Mental health support should be available to both smokers and non‑smokers, with extra attention to the life stresses that can drive both smoking and distress.
Question 4
Is income linked to clinical levels of anxiety and depression?
Very strong pattern
What we did: We recoded PHQ‑4 scores into clinical levels (Normal, Mild, Moderate, Severe) and ran a chi‑square test by income group.
▶ Tap for details
  • Result There was a highly significant association between income and PHQ‑4 category. Low‑income residents were far more likely to fall into the Moderate or Severe range. High‑income residents were more often in the Normal or Mild range.
  • What it shows Financial strain is tightly connected to mental health in our city. This pattern did not appear by chance—it was one of the strongest signals in the whole dataset.
Bottom line: Where income is low, mental health needs are high. Any city‑wide mental health plan should start by listening to and investing in low‑income communities.

Section 2

What This Means for Our City

When we put all four questions together, a clear story emerges: mental health in our city is shaped less by individual “choices” and more by the conditions people are living in every day.

1. Money stress is doing real damage

Across every analysis, income kept showing up. Residents in the lowest‑income bracket were much more likely to have moderate or severe PHQ‑4 scores. This means higher levels of anxiety, worry, and low mood are concentrated where bills are hardest to pay.

2. Access and habits matter, but aren’t the whole story

We expected that difficulty accessing mental health services or smoking would strongly predict PHQ‑4 scores. In this dataset, neither one explained much of the difference. That doesn’t mean they are unimportant—it means that stress, income, and support may be even more powerful.

Healthy cities are not built only on clinics and hotlines. They are built on stable housing, fair pay, safe neighborhoods, and strong relationships—all of which make it easier to stay mentally well and ask for help early.

Section 3

What You Can Do — As a Resident, Neighbor, or Leader

Everyone has a role in building a mentally healthier city. These ideas are starting points, not a full list. Pick one or two that fit your life and community.

For yourself

Notice your stress level and get support early

If your PHQ‑4‑type score (or how you feel day‑to‑day) is creeping upward—more worry, trouble sleeping, feeling down most days—reach out. Talk with a trusted friend, faith leader, or health provider. Stress is common in our city, but you do not have to face it alone.

For your circle

Make “How are you really?” a normal question

Check in with friends, coworkers, and neighbors—especially those juggling money, housing, or caregiving stress. Listening without judgment can be as powerful as formal services, and it can help people seek care before a crisis.

For your neighborhood

Support local spaces that reduce stress

Parks, community centers, libraries, mutual‑aid groups, and cultural spaces all reduce isolation and give people safe places to breathe. Find out what exists in your neighborhood and consider how you can take part or help spread the word.

For policymakers & leaders

Link mental health to economic policies

Our data suggest that economic relief is also mental health care. Policies that improve wages, protect renters, reduce debt, and expand benefits can lower the stress that fuels anxiety and depression across whole neighborhoods.

Section 4

What Is the PHQ‑4 and How Were Scores Used?

PHQ‑4 is a brief, research‑supported tool that asks four questions about feeling nervous, anxious, down, or hopeless over the last two weeks. It does not replace a full diagnosis, but it gives a quick picture of how people are doing emotionally.

In this project, each person received a score from 0 to 12. Higher scores mean more frequent symptoms of anxiety and depression. For some analyses, we grouped scores into four levels:

Normal 0–2 points
Little to no symptoms

Most days feel manageable. Some stress is normal, but it is not overwhelming most of the time.

Mild 3–5 points
Early warning

Some days are harder; you may notice more worry or low mood. This is a good moment to check in with yourself and others.

Moderate 6–8 points
Needs attention

Symptoms are happening more often and affecting daily life. Reaching out to a professional or hot‑line could be very helpful.

Severe 9–12 points
High concern

Strong and frequent symptoms that may make it hard to work, study, or care for yourself or others. This is a signal to seek urgent support.

If you or someone you know feels unsafe or unable to cope, please contact a crisis line, trusted health professional, or emergency service in your area.
